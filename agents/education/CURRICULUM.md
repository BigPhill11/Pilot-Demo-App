# Phil's Financials — Financial Literacy Curriculum (Master Overview)

**Audience:** High school students (13–18), with focus on underserved communities in metro Atlanta
**Partners:** First Tee of Metro Atlanta, Atlanta PAL, prospective Atlanta Public Schools
**Alignment:** Georgia state financial literacy learning standards (now required in GA high schools), with a maintained standards-alignment record
**Design principle:** Decision-driven, not slide-driven. Students learn by making choices that visibly change their virtual kingdom in Bamboo Empire.

## Format

- **8 weeks, one module per week, 30–45 minutes each** — in-platform gameplay plus a short reflection/discussion.
- **Self-directed** inside Bamboo Empire; a facilitator supports the first 2–3 sessions, then hands off via train-the-trainer.
- **Target:** 70%+ program completion.

## Topics covered

Active income and pay · budgeting and financial planning · saving and emergency funds · credit · debt · insurance and risk · investing and compounding · market literacy and financial news · (parallel track) career readiness.

## Week-by-week modules

**Week 1 — Active Income.** Students take their first in-game job and see the difference between gross and net pay land on a real (simulated) paycheck. They learn the two levers of earned income — hours and hourly value — and why skills, not just extra shifts, raise pay. The week ends with a decision: how will you earn more next month?

**Week 2 — Financial Planning.** Budgeting is taught as kingdom-resource allocation: students split limited income across needs, wants, and savings, and watch the kingdom respond to their priorities. They set goals with time horizons and learn to measure their own progress instead of comparing themselves to others.

**Week 3 — Savings.** Students build an emergency fund and experience why saving first beats saving what's left over. Compound growth is made visible: kingdom growth is tied directly to saving consistency, so streaks and automation pay off on screen, not just in theory.

**Week 4 — Credit.** Students learn what a credit score is, what builds it, and what breaks it. The core gameplay is a borrow-or-wait decision whose consequences follow them: today's choice changes the loan terms the kingdom is offered later, making the invisible cost of credit visible.

**Week 5 — Debt.** Students carry a debt and feel its weight — minimum payments versus payoff, interest that compounds against them, and the difference between productive debt (funds growth) and unproductive debt (funds the past). The goal is judgment, not fear: when borrowing helps and when it traps.

**Week 6 — Insurance.** A disaster event hits the kingdom. Students who transferred risk (bought coverage) recover; students who didn't feel the cost. The week teaches risk transfer, premiums and deductibles, and why protection is the price of keeping what you've built.

**Week 7 — Investing.** Students put money to work and let time do the heavy lifting: compounding, starting early, and diversification versus concentration. A multi-year simulated market run tests whether they can stay invested when prices swing.

**Week 8 — Market Literacy.** Students learn how markets work and how to read financial news critically — separating headline hype from signal, understanding what actually moves prices, and building a personal filter so they graduate as skeptical, informed readers rather than headline traders.

## Career Readiness track (parallel or follow-on)

- **Soft skills modules:** interviewing, networking, email and business etiquette, professional habits, personal brand, resume building.
- **Career exploration:** testimonials and profiles from finance professionals showing real paths into the industry.
- **Live practice:** volunteer-run mock interviews and resume reviews.

## Assessment framework (with Georgia Tech)

| Instrument | What it measures |
|---|---|
| Pre/post test per module topic | Knowledge gain per week |
| Time-per-lesson tracking | Engagement and pacing |
| Confidence self-assessment (before/after each module) | Self-efficacy change |
| Career aspiration tracking | Program influence on goals |
| Completion-rate reporting | Program health vs. 70% target |

## Success criteria

70%+ completion · measurable pre/post gains · a standing Georgia standards-alignment record · facilitators running the program independently after train-the-trainer handoff.
